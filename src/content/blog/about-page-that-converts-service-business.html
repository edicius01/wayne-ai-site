Your About Page Is Probably a Placeholder. That's a Problem.

Pull up your About page right now. If it says something like "We're a family-owned business committed to quality service and customer satisfaction," you have a problem — and it's costing you customers.

That sentence says nothing. Every single competitor in your market could write the exact same thing. It doesn't tell a visitor who you are, where you are, how long you've been around, or why they should let you into their home. It's not a story. It's filler. And when a homeowner is trying to decide between you and three other contractors, filler doesn't close the deal.

Your About page isn't a vanity section. It's one of the highest-intent pages on your site — people go there specifically to decide whether to trust you. If it doesn't answer the right questions, they click away.

What Visitors Are Actually Looking for on Your About Page

Here's the thing: nobody lands on your About page to read your mission statement. They're not there to feel inspired. They're there because they're vetting you.

When a homeowner in your area clicks "About Us," they're quietly running through a mental checklist:

  • Are you actually local? Or are you some national company that subcontracts work in my area?
  • Are you legit? Licensed? Insured? Not just some guy with a truck?
  • Will you actually show up? Do you have a track record?
  • Can I trust you in my home? You're asking to come inside. That's a big deal.

Your About page needs to answer all four of those questions — clearly, quickly, and without making the visitor dig for information. If it doesn't, they'll find someone whose page does.

The 5 Things Every Service Business About Page Needs

1. A Real Photo of You and Your Team

Not stock photos. Not a generic image of someone in a hard hat. An actual photo of you — ideally in your truck, at a job site, or with your crew. People hire people. A real face on your About page does more for trust than three paragraphs of copy. If you're a solo operator, a clean headshot works fine. If you have a team, show them. A photo that says "these are the people coming to your home" is one of the highest-trust signals your site can have.

2. How Long You've Been in Business and Your Service Area

"Serving Austin and surrounding areas since 2009" is worth more than an entire paragraph of adjectives. It tells the visitor you're not new, you're established, and you're local. Be specific about your service area — list your city and the surrounding towns or counties you cover. Don't make people guess whether you'll come to them.

3. Why You Started — in 2 to 3 Sentences

You don't need a novel. You just need enough of a story to make you a person, not a faceless business. Did you grow up working with your dad? Did you start the company because you kept seeing homeowners get ripped off? Did you leave a corporate job to build something of your own? Give one specific, honest reason. That's it. It humanizes you instantly.

4. Credentials, Licenses, and Insurance

This one is non-negotiable. Homeowners are literally unlocking their front door for you. "Licensed, bonded, and insured in [State]" isn't a legal formality — it's an objection removed. State it explicitly. If you have certifications (NATE-certified for HVAC, GAF-certified for roofing, etc.), list them. These aren't just credentials — they're proof that you meet a professional standard your competitor down the street might not.

5. A Direct Call to Action

Most About pages just... end. The visitor reads your story, nods, and then has to go figure out how to reach you. Don't make them do that. End your About page with a button or a direct link — "Get a Free Estimate," "Call Us Today," or "Book Online." The person reading your About page is already interested. Give them an immediate next step.

What to Cut Right Now

Just as important as what to add is what to remove. Here's what's hurting your page:

  • Generic adjectives. "Passionate," "dedicated," "quality-focused," "committed to excellence" — these words are meaningless because every business claims them. Cut them all.
  • Vague origin stories. "We started with a dream to serve our community" tells a visitor nothing. Add a specific year, a specific reason, a specific person.
  • Walls of text. Nobody reads dense paragraphs on a service business website. Break it up. Use short paragraphs, bullet points, and headers.
  • Stock photos of strangers. If the people in your photos don't actually work for you, take them down. It feels fake because it is fake, and visitors can tell.

Before and After: See the Difference

Here's what a weak About page intro looks like — and what a strong one looks like instead.

Weak:

"At Summit Plumbing, we are a family-owned and operated business dedicated to providing high-quality plumbing services to homeowners in the area. Our team of experienced professionals is committed to customer satisfaction and delivering exceptional results on every project. We take pride in our work and stand behind everything we do."

Strong:

"I'm Mike Torres, and I've been fixing plumbing in the Denver metro since 2011. I started Summit after 8 years working for a big company where I watched homeowners get overcharged for basic repairs. Now I run a lean crew of four — all background-checked, all licensed — and I show up when I say I will. We're licensed and insured in Colorado, and we serve Denver, Lakewood, Arvada, and Westminster. If you've got a leak or a drain that won't move, call us. We'll pick up."

Same business. Completely different level of trust. The second version is specific, human, and answers every real question a homeowner has. The first could have been written by anyone.

A Better About Page Means More Conversions — Here's Why

When someone calls after reading a strong About page, they're not a cold lead. They already know your name, your face, how long you've been around, and that you're licensed. The trust work is already done. You're not starting from zero — you're starting from halfway there.

That matters because most service businesses lose leads in the gap between interest and contact. Someone finds you, they're interested, and then something breaks the trust — a generic page, a slow response, no answer when they call. Fixing your About page closes one part of that gap. But you also need the follow-through: when a lead calls and you miss it, a fast text-back keeps them from calling your competitor. When someone fills out your contact form, a same-minute response wins the job before they even get quotes.

Trust starts on your About page. It gets reinforced by how fast you respond. Together, they convert more of the traffic your marketing is already driving. And if you're putting work into building your reviews and online credibility, a strong About page makes sure that effort actually pays off when visitors land on your site.

FAQ

How long should my About page be?

Long enough to answer the four trust questions — short enough that someone actually reads it. For most service businesses, that's 200 to 400 words. Don't pad it. Every sentence should earn its place.

Should I hire a copywriter to write it?

Not necessarily. A copywriter can help with polish, but your About page works best when it sounds like you. Write a rough draft in your own voice — even if it's imperfect — and clean it up from there. Authenticity matters more than perfect prose on this page.

Do I need a video on my About page?

A short video (60 to 90 seconds) of you introducing yourself can be extremely effective — but only if you'll actually do it well. A shaky, mumbling video does more harm than good. If you're not comfortable on camera, a strong photo and tight copy will outperform a bad video every time.
