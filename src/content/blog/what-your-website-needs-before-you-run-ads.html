Running ads before your website and follow-up system are ready is like turning on a faucet over a broken bucket. The water — the traffic — comes in, but it goes straight to the floor. You pay for every drop, and almost none of it reaches the glass.

Most service business owners who say "ads don't work" are in exactly this situation. It's not that Google Ads or Facebook Ads are broken. It's that the bucket isn't ready. The traffic shows up, looks around for half a second, and leaves — usually to call a competitor whose site actually answered their questions and made it easy to book.

This checklist is designed to change that. Go through every item before you spend another dollar on paid traffic. Some of these take five minutes to fix. Others take a week. But all of them matter — and skipping them is why most ad campaigns underperform.

The Mindset Shift You Need First

Ads don't create demand. They accelerate it.

When someone clicks your Google ad at 9pm on a Tuesday, they already want what you offer. They have a problem — a broken AC, a leaky pipe, a yard that's out of control — and they are actively looking for someone to solve it. The ad just put your name in front of them at that exact moment.

What happens next is entirely your job. The ad did its part. Now your website, your response speed, your reviews, and your follow-up system have to close the deal. If any of those are broken, the lead evaporates and you've paid for nothing.

Ads send motivated buyers to your front door. What happens at that door is your job — and if the door experience is broken, the ad spend is wasted.

With that framing in mind, here's what needs to be in place before you run a single ad.

1. A Fast Response System

This one is the biggest. If someone fills out your contact form and nobody responds for two hours, that lead is gone. They've already called three other companies. Whoever responded first almost certainly got the job.

Studies on lead response time are consistent: contact a lead within 5 minutes and your odds of converting them are dramatically higher than waiting even 30 minutes. After an hour, most leads are effectively dead.

Before running ads, you need one of two things in place: either a real human who can respond immediately during business hours, or an automated system that fires a text or email the moment someone submits a form — even at midnight. The automated message doesn't have to be fancy. It just has to acknowledge them, set expectations, and ideally ask a qualifying question to keep the conversation moving.

If your current process is "someone checks the email inbox in the morning," you're not ready to run ads yet.

2. A Clear, Specific Offer on Your Landing Page

"We're a local HVAC company serving the greater metro area." That is not an offer. That's a description. It tells the visitor nothing about why they should call you instead of the ten other companies that showed up in the same search.

An actual offer looks like this: "Same-day AC repair. 2-hour response window. 5-star rated with 200+ reviews." That answers the question a motivated buyer is silently asking: Why you and not someone else?

Your landing page — specifically the page your ads send people to — needs to answer that question within the first few seconds. What do you do, where do you do it, how fast can you get there, and what makes you trustworthy? If visitors have to dig to find those answers, most of them won't bother.

3. A Working Phone Number and Contact Option

Check your site on an actual phone right now. Is the phone number clickable? Can someone tap it and immediately start a call, or do they have to copy and paste it manually? On mobile — which is where most of your ad traffic will come from — a non-clickable phone number is a real friction point that kills conversions.

Web chat is the same story. If you have a chat widget on your site, it needs to actually work and actually get responded to. A broken chat or one that routes to an email inbox nobody monitors is worse than having no chat at all — it signals to the visitor that nobody's home.

The goal is to never let a visitor leave without giving you a way to reach them.

4. Online Booking or a Clear Next Step

A lot of service business leads happen outside business hours. Someone's AC goes out at 9pm. A pipe starts leaking on a Saturday. They go to Google, find your ad, click through — and there's no way to do anything except wait until Monday morning to call.

You've lost that lead. They found someone else who had a booking link or a text option that worked at 9pm.

You don't have to have a full scheduling system with calendar sync and automated reminders (though that's the goal). At minimum, you need a clear next step that works around the clock: a booking link, a text-to-start option, or a form that triggers an immediate automated response. Give people something to do when they're ready to hire you, even if that's 11pm on a Sunday.

5. At Least 10 Google Reviews (Ideally 25+)

Ads drive traffic. Reviews close the sale.

Here's what actually happens after someone clicks your ad: they land on your page, glance at it for a few seconds, and then — almost every time — they open a new tab and search your business name to check your Google profile. They want to see stars. They want to read what real customers said about you.

If they find fewer than 10 reviews, or a 3.8-star average, or a bunch of reviews that are two years old with no recent activity, your conversion rate tanks. No amount of good ad copy or slick landing page design fixes a weak review profile.

Before you run ads, work on your reviews. Get to 25+ with a 4.5 or higher average. That's the floor for paid traffic to convert well.

A simple review request system — even just a text sent after every completed job with a direct link to your Google review page — can get you there faster than you think. This is one of the highest-ROI things a service business can do before turning on ads.

6. A Follow-Up System for Leads Who Don't Book Immediately

Ad platforms love to count form submissions as conversions. And technically, they are — someone filled out the form. But if nobody follows up within 5 minutes, that "conversion" is dead in practice.

You need an automated follow-up sequence that fires the moment a form is submitted. At minimum: an immediate confirmation text or email that acknowledges the submission and tells them what happens next. Better: a sequence that follows up if they haven't heard back, asks a qualifying question, and keeps the conversation warm until someone from your team can call.

This is the gap where most small service businesses lose the most leads. The form submission feels like a win, but without fast follow-up, it converts at a fraction of what it should.

7. A Mobile-Optimized Experience

More than half of service business ad traffic comes from mobile devices. In many categories — emergency plumbing, HVAC repair, locksmith — it's closer to 70–80% mobile. Someone with a broken pipe is not sitting at a desktop computer.

Pull up your site on your own phone before you run a single ad. Is the text readable without zooming? Do the buttons work? Does the form actually submit? Is the page loading fast, or is it taking 6 seconds because of an uncompressed image?

A page that looks fine on desktop and broken on mobile will hemorrhage ad spend. Fix this first.

8. Conversion Tracking

If you don't know which ads are generating calls and form fills, you're flying blind. You can't optimize what you can't measure — and without tracking, you have no idea if your Google Search campaign is working while your Facebook campaign is burning money, or vice versa.

Set up conversion tracking for every meaningful action: form submissions, phone calls, booking completions. Google Ads has built-in conversion tracking. For calls, use a dedicated tracking number for each campaign so you can attribute calls back to the source. This data is what lets you double down on what's working and cut what isn't.

The Honest Math on Why This Matters

Let's say you're spending $1,000 a month on Google Ads and converting 1% of clicks into actual leads. That might get you 5 leads a month.

Now imagine you fix the response speed, tighten the landing page offer, get your review count up, and add a follow-up sequence. Conversion rates of 3–5% on well-prepared sites are realistic. At 5%, you're getting 25 leads from the same $1,000.

That's not a minor improvement. That's the difference between an ad campaign that feels like it's not working and one that's driving real revenue — without spending a dollar more on ads.

The systems are the multiplier. The ad spend is just the input.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need all of these before I run any ads, or can I start with a few?

You don't need everything to be perfect, but some items are non-negotiable before you spend money. Fast response (item 1), a clear offer (item 2), and a working phone number (item 3) need to be in place. If those three are broken, ads will consistently underperform regardless of what else you fix. The others — booking, reviews, follow-up, tracking — each add meaningful lift, so get to them as quickly as you can.

My Google Ads agency says my ads are performing well. How do I know if the problem is really my website?

Look at the actual metric that matters: cost per booked job, not cost per click or cost per lead. Agencies often optimize for click-through rate and form submissions because those are easy to report. But if your form submissions aren't turning into booked jobs, the problem is downstream — usually response speed or follow-up — and the agency may not even have visibility into that. Pull your own numbers on how many ad leads actually became customers.

We get good word-of-mouth and referrals. Why does any of this matter?

Referrals and word-of-mouth leads behave differently than cold ad traffic. A referral comes in already trusting you — they'll wait a few hours for a callback, they'll forgive a clunky website, they'll overlook a thin review profile. A cold ad click has none of that. They don't know you, they have options, and they have zero patience. The standards for converting cold traffic are genuinely higher, which is why businesses that are great at referrals sometimes struggle when they move to paid ads.

Is it worth hiring someone to run our ads if we haven't done this checklist yet?

An honest agency will tell you the same thing: get your conversion infrastructure in place first, or you're paying them to send traffic into a system that can't convert it. A good agency can drive traffic. They can't fix your response time, your review count, or your follow-up system — that's on you (or a partner who specializes in that layer). Do the checklist first. Then hire for traffic.
