You're up on a roof. Your hands are dirty. Your phone rings, you can't get to it, and by the time you climb down and call back — fifteen minutes later — they've already called the next guy on the list.

That's not a hypothetical. That's Tuesday. And Wednesday. And every day you're actually doing the work you're supposed to be doing.

The frustrating part isn't missing the call. It's that the lead was real. Someone needed a plumber, a roofer, an HVAC tech — and they called you. They just didn't wait.

A missed-call text-back fixes that. It won't answer the phone for you, but it will respond fast enough to keep that lead from walking out the door. Here's exactly how it works and how to set it up yourself.

What a Missed-Call Text-Back Actually Is

Simple concept: when someone calls your business number and you don't pick up, the system automatically fires them a text message within a few seconds. Not a minute later. Not when you remember to call back. Within seconds — while they're still standing there looking at their phone.

That text says something like: "Hey, sorry I missed you — I'm out on a job. What can I help you with?"

To the caller, it looks like your office responded. They don't know it was automated. They just know someone acknowledged them, and now there's a conversation open. Most people will text back. And once they text back, you've got their attention and their number — and you can close that job when you get a free minute.

Speed is the whole game. Studies consistently show that responding to a lead within 5 minutes makes you 9x more likely to convert them. A text back in under 30 seconds is about as fast as it gets.

This isn't magic. It's just making sure no one gets radio silence when they reach out to you.

What Tools Can Do This

Several platforms offer missed-call text-back out of the box. Most business automation tools — the kind that combine a CRM, messaging, and scheduling in one place — include it as a standard feature. So do some dedicated reputation and messaging platforms built specifically for local service businesses.

When evaluating options, look for three things: a dedicated business phone number, a workflow or automation builder that can trigger on missed calls, and a shared inbox where you or a team member can reply. Those three pieces together are all you need.

If you want to build something more custom, platforms like Twilio let you configure SMS automations from scratch — but that's developer territory and overkill for most service businesses. Stick with tools that have this built in and configurable without code.

How to Set It Up

This isn't going to be a click-by-click tutorial tied to one specific tool — those go stale fast. But here's a clear map of the work involved so you know what you're getting into regardless of which platform you use.

Step 1: Get a Dedicated Business Phone Number

Most automation platforms provision you a local phone number as part of the setup. This is the number your missed-call automation fires from. You can either forward your existing business number to it, or use it as your primary business line going forward. Either works — the key is that your automation platform needs to "see" the call to know it was missed.

Step 2: Set Up Call Forwarding

If you want missed calls on your existing number to trigger the text-back, set up call forwarding so unanswered calls route to your automation platform's number. Most carriers let you do this in your account settings, or you can call your carrier and they'll set it up in five minutes.

Step 3: Build the Automation

Inside your platform, find the workflow or automation builder. Set the trigger to "missed call" — most business automation tools have this as a built-in event. Add an action: send an SMS to the caller's number. Write your message. Save and activate.

That's the core of it. Once it's live, every missed call triggers that text automatically.

Step 4: Test It

Have someone call your number and don't answer. You should receive the automated text within 30 seconds or less. If it doesn't come through, check that your automation is active and that the phone number is correctly connected. Nine times out of ten, if it's not firing, the workflow isn't turned on or there's a number mismatch in settings.

Step 5: Set Up a Conversation Inbox

When someone replies to your automated message, you need a place to see it and respond. Most platforms have a shared inbox — a central place for all your text conversations. You can check it from your computer or a mobile app. If you have someone who helps with scheduling, you can give them access to manage replies. This is where the lead becomes a booked job.

What Your Text Message Should Say

Keep it short. Keep it human. Don't make it sound like a robot wrote it.

Here are three templates you can use or adapt:

Template 1 — Simple and direct:
"Hey, this is [Your Name] with [Business Name]. Sorry I missed your call — I'm out on a job. What can I help you with?"

Template 2 — Slightly warmer:
"Hi! We just missed your call at [Business Name]. We want to make sure you get taken care of — what's going on and how can we help?"

Template 3 — With a light next step:
"Hey, sorry we missed you — we're out in the field right now. Text us what you need and we'll get back to you shortly, or if it's urgent feel free to text URGENT and we'll call you right back."

Don't overthink the message. The goal isn't to close the job in one text. The goal is to start a conversation so they don't move on to your competitor.

What Happens After They Reply

Someone has to handle the reply. That's the honest truth. The automation buys you time and keeps the lead warm, but a human still needs to close it.

Check your inbox the same way you'd check voicemail — a couple times a day, or whenever you have a break between jobs. Reply to active conversations, get the job details, and either book them on the spot or call them back when you're free.

For replies that come in after hours, you can add a follow-up automation that sends a second message if no one replies within a set window — something like: "Just wanted to make sure this didn't fall through the cracks — are you still looking for help?" That kind of follow-up sequence can recover leads that would otherwise go cold overnight.

The automation does the hardest part — responding fast enough to matter. You still close the job. That part hasn't changed.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will people know it's automated?

Most won't, especially if your message sounds natural. Avoid anything that reads like a corporate auto-reply. If someone asks "is this a real person?" — just be honest: "Ha — it's an auto-text so I don't lose you while I'm on a job, but I'm real. What do you need?" People respect that. They just don't want to be ignored.

What if someone calls multiple times? Will they get spammed with texts?

Good automation platforms have controls for this. You can set it to only fire once per caller within a certain time window, so if someone calls twice in an hour they won't get two texts. Make sure that setting is configured when you build your workflow.

Do I need a separate business number, or can I use my cell?

You'll need a number connected to your automation platform for this to work — that's how it detects a missed call and triggers the text. You can keep using your personal cell for everything else and forward your business line into the platform, or make the platform number your main business line. Either way works.

What does something like this cost?

Most business automation platforms that include this feature run $100–$200/month depending on features and message volume, with small per-message costs on top (usually pennies per text). That said, one recovered lead per month typically covers the cost several times over. The math works fast for any active service business.
