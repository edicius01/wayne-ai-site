Most service business owners know they need more Google reviews. They also know they should ask for them. The problem isn't knowledge — it's execution.

Here's what actually happens: your tech finishes a job, the customer seems happy, and there's a half-second window where asking for a review would feel natural. But the tech is already thinking about the next call. The customer is already walking back inside. The moment passes. Nobody says anything. And two weeks later you're wondering why your competitor with worse work has 80 more reviews than you do.

Even when owners do remember to ask, it's inconsistent. One tech asks everyone. Another never asks. You get a handful of reviews when things go well, nothing for months when things get busy, and no real way to predict or grow your rating.

The fix isn't trying harder. It's taking the ask out of anyone's hands entirely.

Why the First 24 Hours Are Everything

There's a window after a completed job where a customer is most likely to leave a review. It's not a week later. It's not when you email them a monthly newsletter. It's in the hours right after the job closes — when the relief of having the problem solved is still fresh, when they remember your tech's name, and when the positive experience hasn't been buried under a dozen other things.

After 24 hours, that window starts closing fast. After 72 hours, most customers have mentally moved on. They're not unhappy with you — they just have other things going on. The review never happens, not because they didn't want to leave one, but because the moment passed.

Automated review requests work precisely because they hit that window every single time, without relying on anyone to remember.

The best time to ask for a review is within an hour of job completion. The second best time is before the day is over. Every hour after that, conversion drops.

What an Automated Review Request Actually Looks Like

The mechanics are simple. When a job is marked complete in your scheduling or CRM software, a text message goes out to the customer automatically — no one has to touch anything. The message is short, sounds like a real person wrote it, and includes a direct link to your Google review page. The customer taps the link, it opens Google, and they leave a review in about 60 seconds.

That's it. No chasing. No awkward conversation at the door. No hoping your tech remembered to mention it.

The automation runs whether you're on a job, in the office, or asleep. It goes out after every single completed job, not just the ones where someone remembered to follow up.

How to Get Your Google Review Link

Before you build anything, you need your direct Google review link. Here's the fastest way to get it:

  • Search for your business name on Google
  • Find your Google Business Profile in the results
  • Click "Get more reviews" in your profile panel (or look for the review link in your Google Business Profile dashboard)
  • Copy that link — it takes customers directly to the review box

You can also search your business name plus "write a review" and Google will usually surface the direct link. Shorten it with a tool like bit.ly if you want cleaner-looking texts, though it's not required.

Keep that link somewhere you can paste it easily — you'll use it in every message template you build.

Step-by-Step: Building the Workflow

The core setup is similar regardless of which platform you use. Here's the logic:

Step 1: Create a new automation workflow triggered when a job or opportunity moves to "completed" or "won" status. If your field service app doesn't have built-in automation, a connector tool like Zapier can pass that signal to whatever messaging platform you're using.

Step 2: Add a short wait. Don't fire the message the instant the job closes — sometimes there's still cleanup happening. A 30-minute to 1-hour delay gives your tech time to wrap up and leave before the customer's phone buzzes.

Step 3: Add an SMS action. Write your message and paste your Google review link directly into the body. Use merge fields to pull in the customer's first name automatically so it doesn't feel generic.

Step 4: Optional follow-up. If you want to increase response rate, add a second branch: wait 48 hours, check if the customer has responded, and if not, send a shorter follow-up. One follow-up is fine. Two starts feeling pushy.

Step 5: Test it. Mark a test job complete and confirm the message fires correctly and the link works. Check it from your phone the way a customer would.

What to Actually Say in the Text

Short wins. Friendly wins. Anything that sounds automated loses. Here are three templates that work:

Template 1 — Simple and direct:
"Hi [First Name] — thanks for having us out today. If we did a good job, it would mean a lot if you left us a quick Google review: [link]. Thanks again, [Your Name] at [Company]"

Template 2 — Slightly warmer:
"Hey [First Name], this is [Tech Name] from [Company]. Just wanted to say it was great working with you today. If you have 60 seconds, a Google review helps us out more than you know: [link]"

Template 3 — Owner voice:
"[First Name] — [Owner Name] here from [Company]. Hope everything looked good today. If you're happy with the work, dropping us a Google review goes a long way: [link]. No pressure either way — appreciate your business."

What Not to Do

  • Don't ask for "5 stars" or a specific rating. Google's terms of service prohibit directing customers to leave positive reviews. Keep it neutral — ask for an honest review, not a good one.
  • Don't send to customers who had a bad experience. If a job went sideways, suppress them from the workflow. Sending a review request to an angry customer is asking for a public one-star complaint. Build a tag or filter to exclude flagged contacts.
  • Don't send more than two messages. One request and one follow-up. After that you're spamming, and customers will start ignoring your number.
  • Make sure your business texting number is properly registered. In the US, sending automated SMS at volume requires A2P 10DLC registration. Most platforms walk you through this — don't skip it or your messages may get filtered by carriers.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it legal to automate review requests?

Yes — automated review requests are fine as long as you're not paying for reviews, requiring them as a condition of service, or directing customers to leave only positive reviews. Asking for honest feedback via text is completely within Google's guidelines. Just don't tell people what to say.

What if a customer clicks the link but doesn't leave a review?

It happens. Some people open the link and get distracted. Your follow-up text 48 hours later catches some of them. Beyond that, there's not much you can do without being pushy. A 25–35% conversion rate on a well-timed SMS is solid — focus on volume and let the math work.

Do I need a CRM to make this work, or can I use my current software?

You need something that can trigger an action when a job is marked complete. If your field service software supports integrations (most do via Zapier or native connections), you can connect it to whatever messaging platform you're using without switching your main system.

How do I keep unhappy customers out of the workflow?

Add a tag to flagged contacts — something like "suppress-review" — and build a filter at the start of your workflow that skips anyone with that tag. Train whoever handles complaints to apply the tag whenever an issue comes up. It takes about five seconds and keeps you from accidentally sending a review request to someone who's already frustrated.
