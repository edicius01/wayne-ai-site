There's a sentence that stops most contractors cold: "I got a cheaper quote."

You've probably heard it more times than you can count. And you know what usually happens next — the owner either drops the price on the spot to save the job, or watches the customer walk out the door. Both feel bad. Both cost money.

But there's a third option. You can hold your price, keep your dignity, and still close a meaningful percentage of those jobs. The key is understanding what's actually happening when a customer pushes back on price — because most of the time, it's not really about the money.

Why Price Objections Usually Aren't Really About Price

When a customer says your price is too high, they're often not saying they can't pay it. They're saying they're not sure you're worth it yet.

Think about it from their side. They're about to hand over a significant amount of money to someone they may have just met. They don't know your work. They don't know if you'll show up on time, clean up after yourself, or stand behind the job if something goes wrong. The price objection is their way of pumping the brakes — of asking for more reassurance without knowing how to ask for it directly.

A price objection is rarely about the number. It's almost always a trust gap in disguise.

Once you understand that, everything changes. You stop treating the objection as a negotiation and start treating it as a conversation — one where your job is to close the trust gap, not lower the number.

The Worst Thing You Can Do: Drop Your Price Immediately

It feels like a solution in the moment. Customer pushes back, you come down a few hundred dollars, they say yes. Problem solved, right?

Not quite. Here's what that customer is actually learning: your first price was padded. You were willing to do the job for less. And next time — or when they refer you to their neighbor — they'll know to push back right away, because it works.

Worse, cutting your price the moment someone questions it signals weakness. Customers don't feel more confident in you when you immediately cave — they feel less confident. If you're willing to drop your price that fast, what else are you willing to cut corners on?

The Three Scenarios Behind "That's Too Expensive"

Scenario 1: They Genuinely Can't Afford It

This happens. Sometimes a customer got a quote that's just outside their real budget, and they're not negotiating — they're stuck. You can usually tell because they seem genuinely deflated, not combative.

How to respond: Acknowledge it without apologizing for your price. Then ask questions. "Can I ask — is there a specific number you're working with?" or "Is there a part of this project that's more urgent than the rest?" The goal isn't to discount — it's to find out if there's a smaller scope that solves their real problem. Maybe they need phase one now and can do phase two in the spring. That's a scope adjustment, not a discount, and it keeps your margin intact.

Scenario 2: They Got a Cheaper Quote Somewhere Else

This is the big one. Someone underbid you — and now they want you to match it.

How to respond: Don't panic. Don't match it. Ask what was included. "I'd be happy to take a look at that. Do you know exactly what's covered in their quote?" Most of the time, the cheaper quote is cheaper for a reason — different materials, no permit, no warranty, a subcontractor with no insurance. When you walk through what your price actually covers, the gap often makes sense.

A script that works: "I want to make sure you're comparing apples to apples. My quote includes [X, Y, Z]. Do you know if theirs covers the same? Because if they're leaving something out, that cost doesn't disappear — it usually shows up later."

Scenario 3: They're Testing You

Some customers push back on price just to see what you'll do. They're not actually convinced the price is wrong. They want to know if you believe in your price, or if you'll fold.

How to respond: Hold your ground, calmly. "I hear you. I want to be honest with you — my price is what it is because that's what it takes to do this job right and stand behind it. I'm not able to come down on it, but I can absolutely walk you through everything it covers if that's helpful." Then stop talking. Let them respond. Silence in this moment works in your favor.

The contractors who hold their price confidently are often the ones customers trust most. Certainty is its own selling point.

The Value Reframe: Shift from Price to Outcome

One of the most effective things you can do in a price conversation is redirect it. Instead of defending your number, shift the focus to what happens if they go with the cheaper option and it goes wrong.

You don't have to be aggressive about it. Just ask a simple question: "If the work doesn't hold up, what does that cost you?"

A homeowner who goes with a roofer who cuts corners isn't saving money — they're gambling with a $15,000 repair down the road. The price they paid becomes the most expensive decision they ever made. You're not trying to scare them. You're helping them think through the full picture.

What Makes Your Price Defensible Before the Objection Comes Up

If customers are constantly pushing back on your price, the problem might not be how you respond to objections. It might be what they know about you before you ever give the quote.

When a customer gets a quote from someone with 200 five-star reviews, a professional proposal, same-day response time, and a clear guarantee — that price feels safe. When they get a quote from someone with three reviews, a handwritten note, and a two-day response time — the same price feels like a risk.

The things that make your price defensible:

  • Reviews: A steady stream of recent, specific reviews does more for your price defense than any sales script. People trust other customers more than they trust you.
  • Fast response: Showing up quickly — to calls, texts, estimate requests — signals professionalism. It tells the customer you'll be just as responsive when the job is done.
  • Clear communication: A detailed, itemized proposal shows the customer exactly what they're getting and makes comparison shopping harder.
  • A real guarantee: If you stand behind your work, say so explicitly. "If anything is wrong, call me and I'll make it right" is worth more than a discount.

What to Do After a Price Objection

A lot of contractors handle an objection and then send the customer off to "think about it" with no next step. That's how jobs disappear. Give them something concrete:

  • A specific follow-up time: "I'll shoot you a message Thursday to see where you landed."
  • A soft deadline if it's real: "I've got a slot opening up in two weeks — if you want to lock it in, just let me know."
  • A follow-up message that recaps what you talked about and restates your value without re-opening the price conversation.

The follow-up is where a lot of jobs are won. Most customers aren't saying no — they're saying "not yet." A professional, timely follow-up keeps you at the front of their mind when they're ready to decide.

Frequently Asked Questions

What if the customer is right and my price really is too high?

That's worth thinking about honestly. If you're losing a high percentage of jobs at your current pricing, it may be worth reviewing your costs and margins. But before assuming price is the problem, look at the other factors — response time, reviews, how you present your quote. Most of the time, the price isn't the issue. The perceived value is.

Is there ever a time I should offer a discount?

Sure — but only when it's on your terms and for a real reason. A discount for a repeat customer, for bundling services, or for a slow season is legitimate. A discount because someone pushed back on your price the first time they heard it is a different thing entirely. One is a business decision. The other is a habit that will cost you.

What if they go with the cheaper guy and it goes badly?

Let them come back. Some of the most loyal customers are ones who chose the cheap option once, regretted it, and found their way back to you. Don't rub it in — just do a great job and they'll remember why your price made sense.

How do I get better at holding my price without feeling uncomfortable?

Practice. Role-play the conversation with someone on your team, or rehearse the key lines out loud until they feel natural. The discomfort usually comes from not knowing what to say next. Once you have a few responses that work, the whole thing gets easier. And every time you hold your price and close the job anyway, your confidence goes up.
