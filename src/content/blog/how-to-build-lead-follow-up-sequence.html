It's 2 PM on a Tuesday. You're flat on your back under a kitchen sink, hands covered in pipe dope, and your phone buzzes. New lead. Someone wants a quote for a water heater replacement. You think: I'll call them back when I surface.

Two hours later, you're loading the van, returning a supplier call, and answering a question from your helper. The lead slips your mind. By Thursday you remember, shoot them a text — and get nothing back. They booked someone else Monday night.

This happens to every service business owner. Not because you don't care. Because you're running jobs, not sitting at a desk with a headset on. The fix isn't more discipline. It's a system that follows up for you the moment a lead comes in — while you're still under that sink.

That system is a lead follow-up sequence. Here's exactly how to build one.

What a Follow-Up Sequence Actually Is

A follow-up sequence is a series of pre-written messages — texts, emails, or both — that go out automatically on a timer after a new lead comes in. The sequence runs on its own. It doesn't need you to remember anything. It doesn't take a lunch break.

The goal is simple: make contact fast, stay visible without being annoying, and give the lead an easy path to book. If they respond at any point, the sequence stops and you take over. If they never respond, the sequence closes out on its own after a set number of days.

Most service businesses don't have one. The ones that do book significantly more jobs from the same number of leads — not because they're better at their trade, but because they're first to respond and last to give up.

Why Most Owners Don't Have This Set Up

It's not laziness. It's priorities. When you're running a crew, managing job sites, ordering materials, and handling callbacks, setting up automation is never the most urgent thing on the list. It keeps getting pushed.

There's also a learning curve. Most CRM and automation tools were built for office-based sales teams, not for a plumber trying to close a bathroom remodel between jobs. But the sequence itself? Once you know the structure, it's not complicated to build. Let's go through it.

The 5-Touch Sequence That Actually Works

This is a sequence you can use as-is. Adjust the wording to match how you actually talk. Keep it short — these aren't emails, they're texts. Short wins.

Touch 1 — Immediate (0 minutes after lead comes in)

Channel: Text

"Hey [First Name], got your message about [service]. Happy to help — what's a good time to connect this week?"

Speed matters more than anything else here. Leads who get a response in under five minutes are dramatically more likely to convert than those who wait an hour. You won't be able to call, but a text going out immediately buys you time and shows you're on it.

Touch 2 — 1 Hour Later (if no reply)

Channel: Text

"Still happy to help whenever works for you — no rush. Just let me know."

One follow-up an hour later is completely normal. It's not pushy. It reminds them you exist without being weird about it.

Touch 3 — Day 2

Channel: Text or Email

"In case it's helpful while you're deciding — [one useful thing about the service, e.g., 'most water heater installs take about 2 hours and we haul the old one away']. Here's our booking link if you want to lock in a time: [link]"

This touch adds value instead of just asking for a response. It also drops your booking link in case they'd rather schedule themselves without talking to anyone — a lot of people prefer that.

Touch 4 — Day 4

Channel: Text

"Don't want to keep filling up your inbox — if you're still looking for a [service type], here's our link to grab a time: [link]. Happy to help whenever."

Acknowledging that you've been following up is disarming. It humanizes the message and gives them a clean way to re-engage without feeling like they owe you an explanation for not responding sooner.

Touch 5 — Day 7

Channel: Text

"Going to close out your inquiry so we don't keep bothering you — but we're here whenever you need us. Take care."

This is your close-out message. Some leads will actually respond to this one because it signals a real ending. It also keeps your pipeline clean and your sequence from running forever.

Five touches over seven days. That's it. Most service businesses never make it past one — if they follow up at all. Running this sequence consistently will put you ahead of the majority of your competitors without you doing anything differently on the job.

What Tools to Use

You need a CRM or automation platform with workflow capability. The specific tool matters less than finding one that can handle these three things: trigger a sequence when a lead comes in, send SMS automatically, and stop the sequence the moment someone replies.

Most all-in-one business automation platforms built for local service businesses include all of this. Look for one that has a visual workflow builder — it makes the logic much easier to set up without needing technical knowledge. If your current setup can't send texts natively, a connector tool like Zapier can bridge it to a texting service.

For most small service businesses, a single platform that combines CRM, texting, and automation is the cleanest path. Fewer moving parts means fewer things to break.

How to Set Up the Workflow

Here's the basic flow inside any automation platform:

  1. Set your trigger. This is what starts the sequence — a form submission from your website, a missed call, a web chat message, or a new contact added to your CRM. You can have multiple triggers feeding the same workflow.
  2. Add your first action — send SMS. Pull in your Touch 1 message. Use a merge field for first name so it personalizes automatically.
  3. Add a Wait step. Set it to 1 hour. Then add Touch 2 (SMS).
  4. Keep adding Wait + Action steps for each touch, using the delays listed above.
  5. Set your stop condition. This is the most important part — covered in the next section.

The whole build takes about 45 minutes once you know what you're doing. If you've never opened your platform's workflow builder before, budget an afternoon.

The Stop Condition: Don't Skip This

A follow-up sequence that keeps running after someone has already replied is one of the fastest ways to kill a deal. Imagine a lead responds to Touch 1, you have a great conversation, they're scheduled — and then they get Touch 4 two days later acting like they never heard from you. It makes you look disorganized at best, broken at worst.

Set a goal condition or stop trigger so that if the contact replies to any message — or books an appointment, or their status changes to "Booked" — the sequence stops immediately.

Most platforms handle this with an "if/else" branch or a goal event. If they replied, end the workflow. If not, continue to the next message. This one step is what separates a sequence that feels like a human built it from one that feels like a robot that doesn't know it's been answered.

What Makes Messages Convert vs. Feel Spammy

The difference is almost always length and pressure. Here's a quick contrast:

Spammy: "Hi [First Name]! We're following up on your recent inquiry. Our team of certified professionals is ready to assist you with all your plumbing needs. Please don't hesitate to reach out. We offer competitive pricing and financing options. Click here to schedule today!!!"

Converts: "Hey Mike, still happy to help with that leak — just let me know when works."

Short. Human. No exclamation points. No bragging. No pressure. Write these messages the way you'd text a neighbor, not the way a corporate marketing team would write a drip campaign. The more it sounds like a real person wrote it, the better it performs.

Frequently Asked Questions

What if I already have a CRM — do I need to switch?

Not necessarily. If your current CRM has workflow automation and can send SMS natively, you can build this same sequence in it. The trigger types and step names will look different, but the logic is identical. Check whether you have an SMS integration active before you assume it'll work — many CRMs handle contacts fine but aren't actually set up to send messages.

Is seven days too long? Won't they have moved on?

Some will. But a meaningful percentage of leads are still undecided at day four or five — they got busy, they're comparing quotes, or they're waiting on a spouse to weigh in. The sequence costs you nothing to run, so there's no reason to cut it short. Day seven's close-out message regularly gets responses from people who've been sitting on it.

What if I get a lot of leads and this floods people with texts?

A well-built stop condition handles this. Once someone books or replies, they're out of the sequence. If you're generating high lead volume, add a filter that checks whether a contact already has an active workflow before enrolling them again to prevent duplicates.

Do I need to register my phone number for business texting?

Yes, and this is often overlooked. If you're sending texts at any real volume from a business number in the US, you need to go through A2P 10DLC registration (Application-to-Person, 10-digit long code). Most platforms walk you through this. It takes a few days to process. Skip it and your messages will get filtered or blocked by carriers — which defeats the entire purpose. Set this up before you go live.
