You sent the estimate. You put together fair numbers, showed up on time, answered their questions. Then… nothing. A few days pass. You figure they went with someone cheaper. You move on.

But did they actually go with someone else?

Most of the time, no. Most estimates don't get rejected — they get forgotten. The homeowner got busy. They had a family thing come up. They're still comparing two or three bids and keep putting off the decision. The project is real, the budget is there, and they still need the work done.

The contractor who follows up gets the job. The one who waits by the phone doesn't.

This post is going to show you exactly how to follow up after an estimate — when to reach out, what to say, and how many times to try before you let it go. You'll get real templates you can copy and use today.

Why Most Owners Don't Follow Up (And Why That's Costing Them)

Ask most service business owners why they don't follow up on estimates and you'll hear the same things:

  • "I don't want to seem desperate."
  • "If they wanted to hire me, they would've called."
  • "I don't know what to say without sounding pushy."
  • "I've got enough work right now anyway."

Some of that is ego. Some of it is genuine awkwardness. And some of it is just not having a system, so it never happens.

Here's the problem: assuming silence means no is one of the most expensive assumptions you can make. A homeowner who doesn't call back isn't necessarily choosing someone else — they're often just stuck in their own life, waiting for someone to make it easy to move forward.

Following up isn't pushy. Done right, it's helpful. You're giving them what they need to make a decision.

The Truth About Estimate Conversion Rates

Most service businesses convert somewhere between 20% and 40% of their estimates into jobs. The ones with a real follow-up process push that number significantly higher — some into the 50–60% range — without changing their prices at all.

The difference between a 30% close rate and a 55% close rate usually isn't price. It's follow-up. The second or third touch is where the job gets won.

Think about it from the customer's side. They got three bids. They liked yours. But yours was the middle price, and they're not sure if the cheapest guy is fine or if spending a little more is worth it. They keep meaning to call you back and ask a couple questions, but it slips. Then a week goes by. Then two. Now it's been so long they feel awkward reaching out.

One short message from you breaks that awkwardness and gets the conversation going again.

The Ideal Follow-Up Sequence

Here's the timing that works. Don't overthink it — just follow the sequence.

Touch 1: 24–48 Hours After the Estimate

This is your most important follow-up. The job is fresh in their mind. You're still a real person to them, not just a piece of paper on the kitchen counter.

Do not send a generic "just checking in" message. That helps nobody. Instead, add something useful — a quick note that answers a question you noticed they had, a heads-up about your schedule, or a reminder of something you discussed on-site.

Email template:

Subject: Quick note on your [project type] estimate

Hi [Name],

Just wanted to follow up on the estimate I sent over for your [project]. I know there's a lot to think through with a project like this.

One thing I forgot to mention on-site: [add one relevant detail — a material choice, timeline note, or warranty point]. Happy to answer any questions before you decide.

You can reply here or call/text me at [number]. No pressure either way.

[Your name]
[Company]

Text template:

Hey [Name], this is [Your Name] from [Company]. Just following up on your estimate from [day]. Happy to answer any questions — just text or call whenever works for you.

Touch 2: Day 5–7

If you haven't heard back, send a second message. This one should acknowledge that they're probably comparing options and give them a reason to choose you — without being defensive about your price.

Email template:

Subject: Something that might help you decide

Hi [Name],

I know you're probably still weighing your options on the [project]. That's completely fair — it's a real investment.

One thing that's worth knowing: [add one value point — experience with this type of job, a warranty you offer, how you handle cleanup, whatever genuinely sets you apart]. That's something I make a point to do on every job.

If price is a concern, I'm also happy to talk through the scope and see if there's a way to phase the work that fits your budget better.

Either way, no pressure. I just want to make sure you have what you need to make a good decision.

[Your name]
[Company]
[Phone]

Touch 3: Day 14 — The Close-Out Message

This one works better than most people expect. It signals that you're moving on, which creates a small sense of urgency — and for people who were genuinely interested but kept procrastinating, it's often the message that gets them to finally respond.

Email template:

Subject: Closing out your estimate

Hi [Name],

I've been holding some availability open in case you wanted to move forward on the [project], but I don't want to assume either way.

If the timing isn't right or you've decided to go a different direction, no problem at all — just let me know and I'll close this out on my end.

If you do want to move forward or have any questions, I'm still happy to help. Just reply here or give me a call.

Hope the project comes together well for you.

[Your name]
[Company]
[Phone]

The Subject Line Problem

A follow-up email that never gets opened is the same as no follow-up at all. Most follow-up subject lines are either boring or feel like obvious marketing, so they get ignored.

Here are three subject lines that actually get opened:

  • "Quick note on your [project type] estimate" — specific, sounds like a real person wrote it
  • "Something that might help you decide" — curiosity without being clickbait
  • "Closing out your estimate" — sounds like a genuine update, not a sales pitch

Avoid subject lines like "Following up on my estimate" or "Just checking in" — they signal immediately that nothing new is being offered.

Call, Text, or Email — How to Choose

  • Small jobs (under $500): Text is usually fine. Fast, low pressure, easy to respond to.
  • Mid-size jobs ($500–$5,000): Start with email or text, but if they don't respond to two touches, try a call. It's worth the extra minute.
  • Large jobs ($5,000+): Call first when possible, follow up with email. A job this size deserves a real conversation.
  • Match how they contacted you: If they filled out a web form, email feels natural. If they called or texted initially, stick to text for follow-ups.

Handle the Price Objection Before It Kills the Deal

A lot of estimates die not because the customer thought you were too expensive, but because they weren't sure why your price was what it was. They didn't want to seem rude by asking, so they just went quiet.

You can handle this proactively with one or two lines in your Day 5–7 follow-up:

"I know our estimate might not be the lowest you received. The difference usually comes down to [one specific thing — materials, warranty, not cutting corners on prep work]. I'm happy to walk through the numbers if that would help."

That one line opens the door. You're not being defensive. You're just making it safe for them to ask.

Most customers don't reject your price — they just don't understand what they're getting for it. A single sentence in your follow-up can change the whole conversation.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many times should I follow up before I let it go?

Three touches is the right number for most service businesses. Any more than that and you risk annoying someone who made their decision and just hasn't told you. The sequence above — 24–48 hours, Day 5–7, Day 14 — covers you without being overbearing. After the Day 14 message, let it go. If they come back later, great. If not, you've done everything reasonable.

What if they respond and say they're still thinking about it?

Acknowledge it without pressure: "No problem at all — take the time you need. I'm here whenever you're ready." Then give it another 7–10 days before sending one final gentle check-in. At that point, use the close-out message and mean it.

Is it okay to follow up by text even for bigger jobs?

Yes, especially if that's how they've been communicating with you. Keep the tone professional and make sure your contact info is easy to find. For jobs over $5,000, following up with a call in addition to text shows you take the project seriously — and gives you a chance to hear what they're actually thinking.

What if I gave a verbal estimate and didn't send anything in writing?

Send a written summary before your first follow-up. Even a simple email that outlines the scope, price, and timeline gives the customer something concrete to reference — and it makes your follow-up feel natural rather than random. It also protects you if there's ever a dispute about what was quoted.
